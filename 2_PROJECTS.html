Food Printing System

RSE3106 Systems Engineering Project 2

june 1, 2024 ashley ling

project overview

Our project began with enhancing 3D and 2D food printing systems for better customization of food shapes, colors, and textures. We then pivoted to addressing accessibility for visually impaired individuals by integrating Braille embossing and NaviLens technology into food printing. Binder inkjet technology emerged as the best solution, enabling us to print tactile, Braille-like text on food products safely and efficiently. This approach improves accessibility while utilizing existing food printing infrastructure with minimal modifications.

Food printing technology

3D Food Printers: printers operate by extruding food materials through a nozzle, which moves along the X, Y, and Z axes to create intricate shapes and structures.

2D Food Printers: Similar to traditional inkjet printers, these deposit food-grade inks onto flat surfaces, creating detailed images and patterns.

Problem Domain exploration

Information Accessibility for The Visually Impaired

More than 1.1 billion people worldwide are visually impaired, and 9 out of 10 of them report difficulties in reading information and labels. This issue arises because printed information is often in small text, disregarding the needs of the visually impaired population. Accessing information such as expiry dates, dietary details, and usage instructions is essential for making informed decisions.

Looking into Existing Solutions

Braille Embossers: These devices create tactile text readable by touch, offering enhanced memory retention without requiring digital devices. However, they need specialized equipment and skills.

NaviLens: This technology uses visual codes that smartphones can scan to provide audio descriptions. While it is user-friendly and capable of storing vast information, it is dependent on smartphones, which might not be convenient or accessible for everyone.

Preliminary Concept

We realized that the primary concept of this system was the extrusion of materials to elevate the surface essential for creating tactile feedback, which is the core of Braille's functionality. Based on this concept, we explored into three main technologies: thermal ink, UV curable ink, and binder inkjet technology.

Thermal Ink Uses heat to cure the printed material, ensuring durability and tactile feedback similar to Braille embossing. However, heat posed risks of damaging food products, making this method less viable.

UV Ink Cures material quickly using ultraviolet light. This method required system modifications (incorporating UV light sources) and raised concerns about potential chemical residues on food.

Binder Inkjet Involves using a binding agent to solidify printed materials. By carefully controlling binder and material interactions, we achieved the desired tactile feedback and precision required for Braille text while maintaining food safety. This method also allowed us to utilize existing food printing infrastructure with minimal modifications.
